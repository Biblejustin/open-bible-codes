## Why This Source Matters

This article is useful because it states skeptical objections that our final
reports need to answer directly. It also names popular ELS claim families and
failure modes:

- Paul Crouch / Grant Jeffrey / Hal Lindsey public advocacy context;
- Aish HaTorah Discovery Seminar context;
- Rabin assassination claims;
- AIDS, Holocaust, and Hitler claims;
- Muslim, Jewish, and cultic numeric/ELS apologetic uses;
- claims about Allah, Muhammad, and Jesus as false Messiah;
- broad search directions, arbitrary spacing, after-the-fact interpretation,
  and nonvoweled Hebrew ambiguity.

## Methodology Warnings To Preserve

The article argues that ELS searches become non-scientific when the searcher
can vary too many knobs after seeing the result. For our project, these become
explicit design constraints:

- lock term spellings before running a claim test;
- lock corpus, normalization, direction, skip range, row width, and matrix
  layout before looking at hits;
- distinguish exact ELS paths from loose matrix clusters;
- distinguish hidden-path-only from same-surface or related-surface rows;
- keep full-span searches, but report legal search-space size;
- count all tested rows and apply multiple-testing correction;
- use non-Bible and shuffled controls;
- record version/source distribution instead of flattening results;
- keep post-screen follow-ups labeled as post-screen;
- require archived source timing for modern-event prediction claims;
- treat consonantal ambiguity as a formal spelling-audit issue.

## Claim-Catalog Implication

This source should not add reproduced claims. It should strengthen the claim
catalog's standard:

1. no public-media claim can move above `under_specified` without exact spelling
   and geometry;
2. no post-screen row should be described as prospective;
3. no modern-event claim should be treated as predictive unless source timing is
   archived before the event;
4. no matrix/cluster result should be interpreted without a declared cluster
   statistic and matched controls.

## Cautions

- The article is a critique, so it should shape controls and interpretation,
  not supply positive ELS targets by itself.
- Some examples are English descriptions of Hebrew ambiguity; exact Hebrew
  forms require separate spelling audit.
- The article's strongest practical value is methodological pressure: if our
  reports cannot answer these objections, they are not ready for claim language.
